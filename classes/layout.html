CSS Rules: Yes, it rules, but let's talk about rules

Review
The web is a vast system built on the internet. It was designed for scientists and researchers to share information, and today it's used by zillions of people for all sorts of purposes.

Information on the web is made available by organizing it into web sites. A web site is made up of basic pieces called web pages.

One of the most important parts of the web is a computer program called a browser. Your browser understands 3 important programming languages that allow you to tell it what to do: HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

HTML describes the content of a web page. CSS tells the browser about the appearance of the content. JavaScript tells the browser how the page should behave.

The Problem
The basic job of your browser is to show you web pages. More precisely, HTML and CSS (and, to a lesser extent, JavaScript) were designed to allow web pages to be described, and your browser's job is to understand what the HTML and CSS you feed it means, and then carry out those instructions so that you see the page properly.

Back a long long time ago, when printing on paper was first invented, people used little blocks — almost like alphabet blocks — with letters and symbols. To print a story or a book, lots and lots of those blocks would be put together to make all the words of the book. A frame would hold those letters in place, and then they could print pages.

Eventually, big giant machines were developed to make printing faster. Controlling those machines required a lot of special skills. When people made newspapers or things like that, usually there would be people who wrote stories, people who decided where the stories would go in the newspaper (and other things like headlines and drawings), and still other people who would actually tell the printing machinery what to do.

The people who wrote stories and decided how the newspaper should look developed a system, called markup, that would tell the printing expert what they wanted to be done. The person controlling the printing machinery didn't really care what the newspaper said. They just had to worry about carrying out the instructions specified by the markup.

HTML Is Markup
When the researcher who designed HTML originally thought about the problem, therefore, there was already a long history of markup. In fact, there were lots of markup systems already designed for computers. One of those, called SGML, was popular in the 1980's.

Starting from SGML, then, Mr. Berners-Lee created a much-simplified variation, and that was the beginning of HTML.

How Are Antique Printers Like HTML?
Think back to those "frames" of alphabet blocks we talked about. Think about what newspapers look like. If you squint your eyes and just think about the shapes on a page of newsprint, you see boxes of information going down the page, one on top of the other. Inside the boxes, letters, spaces, and punctuation just sort of "flow", line by line through the boxes.

(We'll talk about the columns that go across the page. For now, we'll concentrate on boxes piled one on top of the other, going down the page.)

Almost all computer-oriented markup systems are based on that simple idea: boxes of flowing text, piled on top of each other. HTML is like that. The basic thing you do in your HTML programs is organize your content, like text for stories or poems and illustrations and pictures, into boxes.

Wait I Thought This Was About CSS
It is about CSS. The job of HTML is to organize your content into boxes. CSS says what the boxes (and the content inside the boxes) should look like. The two work together to determine how a web page looks.

However, HTML is "older" than CSS. That means that when HTML first existed, there was no such thing as CSS. HTML allows you to put your content into boxes such that a browser can know how to show it even without CSS. The way that works is based on a family of tags (remember tags from last week?) that make different kinds of boxes.

Paragraphs
Last week we looked at a particular kind of box, the paragraph box. Paragraphs are a pretty basic kind of box. Recall that to make a paragraph, we use some HTML that looks like this: <p> This is a lovely paragraph. I think it may be my favorite paragraph ever. </p> There are other kinds of boxes too. For example, there's a family of "header" tags, "h1" through "h6", that can be used to identify chapter and section titles: <h1>My Summer Vacation</h1> The browser knows that second-level headings should be a little smaller than chapter titles, etc.

There's another kind of box that's a sort-of "generic" box. The "div" tag makes a box, but it's a box without any pre-arranged meaning. In other words, the browser knows it's a box, so it knows it stacks on top of other boxes, but it doesn't associate it with any particular kind of information.

(Can you guess how it will be possible to control what content in a "div" tag looks like?)

Box Me In
Inside boxes, we find inline content. The simplest inline content is just blah blah blah text: <p> This summer was fun. I went to a beach. My little brother got sunburned and a fish bit him but so what. I had lots of cotton candy. </p> The browser knows that simple text should just be strung along, letter by letter. It also knows to break at the edge of the page and start over on the next line.

There are some tags that don't make boxes. Instead, they tell the browser that a stretch of inline content has some overall relationship. One example is the title of a movie, which is traditionally shown as italic text: <p> Yesterday my sister went to see <i>Forever Bieber</i> and ever since she's been hyperventilating with excitement. </p> The "i" tag tells the browser to use italic letters. (Wait a minute — I thought it was CSS's job to control appearance!)

Just like with blocks, there's a "generic" inline content tag that lets content be grouped without any particular reason. It's called "span". (How much do you think you could accomplish with just "div" and "span" in an HTML page?)

Every Tag Is Special
Well, not really. So far all we've seen in CSS is that we can make all paragraphs turn purple, and stuff like that. I don't know about you, but sometimes I like certain paragraphs to be different.

HTML and CSS, thankfully, give us lots of neat-o ways to make our plain anonymous tags more "special". The first way we'll talk about is the idea of categorizing or "classifying" our tags. In HTML, you can give a tag one or more "classes" to say, for example, what kind of paragraph you've got. You do it by putting some extra information in the opening tag of the paragraph: <p class="poem"> Turtle, turtle, on the ground Pink and shiny, turn around. </p> Now we've got a way to say, in HTML, "Hey, Browser! This is a paragraph, but it's a poem paragraph, unlike all my other boring paragraphs." Great! Except ... how do we use CSS to tell the browser how our poems should look?

In CSS, remember that each rule consists of a "selector" and a "rule body". So far we've seen really simple selectors that just mean, "every p tag in the whole page". Well we can now say, "every p tag that's a poem" like this: p.poem { white-space: pre; } The class name "poem" is added to the tag name ("p") with a period character. So "p.poem" means, to the browser, that the rule body applies not to every single "p" tag, but only to "p" tags that are marked as being poems!

Thinking Inside And Outside The Box
Think about that imaginary newspaper again. Sometimes, content is broken down in a more complicated way than just a list of boxes. Sometimes there are boxes inside other boxes.

In HTML, you can put a tag inside another tag: <div> <p> This is a short sentence. </p> <p> Another paragraph. </p> </div> That's a box inside a box!

Next time we'll talk about some crazy stuff we can do with boxes inside boxes, classes, and CSS.
